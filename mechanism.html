The Central Mechanism

Excess Momentum

Explaining the productivity slowdown with too much innovation

The Puzzle

Three facts about the post-2005 U.S. economy are individually well-documented but jointly puzzling. Total factor productivity growth has halved — from roughly 1.5% per year to 0.5%. R&D expenditure as a share of GDP rose from 2.5% to 3.5%, and patent applications tripled. Meanwhile, R&D-intensive NASDAQ firms have earned a persistent 4–6 percentage point annual equity premium unexplained by standard risk factors.

More resources are flowing into invention. Less is coming out as measured productivity. And markets are rewarding innovators handsomely. Something is deeply wrong in the mapping from private to social returns to innovation.

Technology Waves and the Learning Curve

We model technology diffusion as a series of waves. Each wave begins with a new invention and a low initial maturity level. As firms and workers use the new technology, maturity grows logistically — workers develop tacit knowledge, managers restructure processes, complementary investments accumulate. A more mature wave creates larger learning economies: effective productivity rises with experience.

This is where the real economic payoff lives — not at the moment of invention, but deep into the diffusion process, when the technology has been absorbed.

The Wave-Reset Problem
Bars represent wave maturity over time. When a new innovation arrives (gaps), maturity resets to near zero. Frequent resets prevent the economy from ever reaching the high-maturity harvesting phase where productivity gains are largest.

The Wave-Reset Externality

When a firm invents a new technology, the current wave is reset: maturity drops back to a small initial value. The patent holder captures the monopoly rents from the new wave but does not internalize the social loss from truncating the diffusion of the previous wave.

This is a negative externality of innovation — a form of creative destruction that destroys not only existing vintage capital (the standard channel) but also accumulated learning capital. When the innovation environment is very fertile, firms innovate too frequently, perpetually resetting immature waves and never allowing diffusion to mature.

TFP growth is paradoxically low precisely because innovation effort is high.

Financial Amplification

High returns to innovative firms relax equity-financing constraints, channeling yet more capital into R&D and pushing realized innovation rates further above the social optimum. This self-reinforcing loop means that shocks to financial market conditions — deeper venture capital markets, low interest rates, rising institutional appetite for growth equity — can push the economy into the excess-innovation regime even when the underlying technology environment has not changed.

Key Analytical Results

Optimal innovation intensity is interior

Too much innovation is as bad as too little. Long-run technology growth is hump-shaped in innovation intensity.

Competitive equilibrium generates excess innovation

For sufficiently fertile environments (high λ), the market innovates more than the social planner would. The wedge widens with learning economies and financial amplification.

TFP growth is non-monotone in innovation fertility

Measured productivity first rises, then falls as the innovation environment becomes more fertile — directly delivering the paradox.

The equity premium reflects rents, not risk

R&D-intensive firms earn a return premium driven by capitalized patent quasi-rents, orthogonal to standard CAPM factors — consistent with the stable NASDAQ alpha.

The Technology Treadmill

The timeline below illustrates the successive technology waves a typical firm has been asked to adopt, learn, and then abandon or overhaul in just twenty years. The average ERP replacement cycle is 10+ years, but the waves arrive every 2–4 years. The mismatch is the core of the problem.

2000–2005
On-premises ERP (SAP, Oracle). Multi-year implementations costing $10M–$500M+.
2005–2010
Web 2.0, early SaaS (Salesforce, Workday). Shift from licensed software to subscriptions.
2010–2013
Big Data (Hadoop, MapReduce). "Data Scientist" invented as a job title. Data lakes built.
2013–2016
Cloud migration (AWS, Azure, Spark). On-premises abandoned. "Cloud Architect" emerges.
2016–2019
Machine learning platforms (TensorFlow, PyTorch). ML Engineer role created. Most projects never reach production.
2019–2021
Cloud-native everything (Kubernetes, microservices, MLOps). Monolithic apps rewritten.
2022–2023
Generative AI / LLMs. "Prompt Engineer" invented. 95% of GenAI POCs fail (MIT).
2024–2025
AI-native platforms, copilots, agentic AI. Firms abandon "build" for "buy." Prior ML investments written off.
2026+
Autonomous agents, ERP abstraction. Gartner predicts 40% of agentic AI projects canceled by 2027. The cycle continues.

Policy Implications

The standard prescription — subsidize R&D, extend patents, deepen capital markets — would make the problem worse. What is needed are mechanisms that slow down technological displacement during periods of high innovation fertility: shorter patents during booms, taxes on wave-resetting inventions that scale with the maturity of the technology they displace, and friction in the financial pipeline channeling equity capital into R&D.

These are blunt instruments. But the alternative is to keep celebrating innovation inputs while watching productivity outputs decay — to keep marveling at the speed of the treadmill while the runner stands still.
