The Central Mechanism

Excess Innovation Momentum

Learning Capital and the Productivity Paradox

The Puzzle

Two facts about the post-2005 U.S. economy are individually well-documented but jointly puzzling. Total factor productivity growth has halved — from roughly 1.5% per year to 0.5%, where it has remained through 2023. At the same time, R&D expenditure as a share of GDP rose from 2.5% to 3.5%, patent applications tripled, and venture capital deal value surged from under $50 billion to over $330 billion.

More resources are flowing into invention. Less is coming out as measured productivity. In standard Schumpeterian models, an increase in innovation inputs should raise growth unless offset by declining research productivity. The standard diagnosis attributes the slowdown to "ideas getting harder to find." We propose a different mechanism: the problem is not that research has become less productive, but that it has become too productive.

Technology Waves and the Learning Curve

We model technology diffusion as a series of waves. Each wave begins with a new invention and a low initial maturity level. As firms and workers use the new technology, maturity grows logistically — workers develop tacit knowledge, managers restructure processes, complementary investments accumulate. A more mature wave creates larger learning economies: effective productivity rises with experience.

This is where the real economic payoff lives — not at the moment of invention, but deep into the diffusion process, when the technology has been absorbed.

The Wave-Reset Problem
Bars represent wave maturity over time. When a new innovation arrives (gaps), maturity resets to near zero. Frequent resets prevent the economy from ever reaching the high-maturity harvesting phase where productivity gains are largest.

The Learning-Capital Externality

When a new technology displaces an incumbent, the productive know-how that workers and firms have accumulated — failure-mode expertise, complementary skills, organizational routines, supplier ecosystems — becomes largely obsolete. This is not a standard adjustment cost or a redistributional loss to incumbents: it is the destruction of real process knowledge that made the old technology productive.

The innovator captures a patent prize but does not compensate society for the lost learning capital. The social value of innovation must subtract this destruction cost, while the private value does not. This wedge between private and social incentives is what can make innovation privately excessive.

The wedge is largest when the incumbent technology is mature, because a mature technology has the most accumulated learning to lose. At sufficiently mature states, the social value of innovation turns negative — the planner would prefer to continue harvesting the existing technology — while the market keeps innovating at a constant, state-independent rate.

Key Analytical Results

1. Social value of innovation declines with maturity

As the incumbent technology matures, it has more learning capital to lose. The social value of innovation falls — and can turn negative — even while the private patent prize remains constant.

2. Long-run growth is hump-shaped in innovation intensity

Too little innovation leaves the economy on exhausted technologies. Too much traps it on perpetually immature ones. The growth-maximizing innovation rate lies in between.

3. The competitive equilibrium innovates excessively

The market hazard is state-independent and increasing in research productivity. The planner's hazard is state-dependent and can fall to zero on sufficiently mature technologies. The reset-state wedge is universal across all parameter configurations.

4. The TFP paradox

Once equilibrium innovation passes the growth-maximizing level, higher research productivity raises the frequency of technology resets while lowering productivity growth. More R&D, more patents — less growth.

The Technology Treadmill

The timeline below illustrates the successive technology waves a typical firm has been asked to adopt, learn, and then abandon or overhaul in just twenty years. The average ERP replacement cycle is 10+ years, but the waves arrive every 2–4 years. The mismatch is the core of the problem.

2000–2005
On-premises ERP (SAP, Oracle). Multi-year implementations costing $10M–$500M+.
2005–2010
Web 2.0, early SaaS (Salesforce, Workday). Shift from licensed software to subscriptions.
2010–2013
Big Data (Hadoop, MapReduce). "Data Scientist" invented as a job title. Data lakes built.
2013–2016
Cloud migration (AWS, Azure, Spark). On-premises abandoned. "Cloud Architect" emerges.
2016–2019
Machine learning platforms (TensorFlow, PyTorch). ML Engineer role created. Most projects never reach production.
2019–2021
Cloud-native everything (Kubernetes, microservices, MLOps). Monolithic apps rewritten.
2022–2023
Generative AI / LLMs. "Prompt Engineer" invented. 95% of GenAI POCs fail (MIT).
2024–2025
AI-native platforms, copilots, agentic AI. Firms abandon "build" for "buy." Prior ML investments written off.
2026+
Autonomous agents, ERP abstraction. Gartner predicts 40% of agentic AI projects canceled by 2027. The cycle continues.

Policy Implications

The model does not call for a blanket reduction in R&D or a moratorium on creative destruction. Innovation remains the engine of frontier growth. The point is that the efficient pace of replacement is state- and sector-contingent: in environments with steep learning curves, private incentives push displacement beyond the socially desirable rate.

On the innovation side, uniform R&D subsidies are too blunt. The learning-capital externality varies across sectors — large where learning curves are steep and technology turnover is fast, small where innovation primarily opens new product space. Subsidies that favor incremental process improvement within an existing technology generation complement the learning process rather than disrupting it.

The more novel policy lever operates on the learning side. Policies that speed up adoption — raising the effective adoption speed — or accelerate the accumulation of process knowledge reduce the learning lag and shift the growth-maximizing hazard to the right. Concrete instruments include workforce retraining and competence formation, knowledge-transfer platforms, and educational investments in adaptable human capital. The goal is not less innovation but faster learning.
