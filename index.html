The Technology Treadmill

Twenty-five years of waves that never matured

Each wave demanded new hires, new vendors, and new organizational processes. Most firms never fully harvested any single wave before the next arrived. The average ERP replacement cycle is 10+ years — the waves below arrive every 2–4.

–2005
On-premises ERP
SAP, Oracle, PeopleSoft. Multi-year implementations costing $10M–$500M+. Massive organizational change. Many failed before delivering returns (Hershey's, Nike, Waste Management).

Salesforce, Workday. Shift from licensed software to subscriptions. New vendor relationships. CRM and HR moved to cloud first — before ERP implementations were complete.

Big Data
Hadoop, MapReduce, NoSQL. "Data Scientist" — a title barely used before 2010 — became one of the hottest jobs in America. Firms built data lakes. New infrastructure teams hired and trained.

Cloud Migration & Analytics
AWS, Azure, Spark, Tableau. On-premises data centers abandoned. Spark replaced Hadoop. BI teams retooled. "Cloud Architect" emerged as a mandatory hire. Data lakes rebuilt in the cloud.

TensorFlow, PyTorch, AutoML. Companies built internal ML teams and invested in GPU infrastructure. Most ML projects never reached production. The ML Engineer role was created from scratch.

Kubernetes, microservices, MLOps. Monolithic applications rewritten. "Platform Engineer" and "MLOps Engineer" emerged as distinct roles. Containerization became mandatory. Prior ML investments reframed as technical debt.

The Central Mechanism

What if the economy innovates too much?

Each new technology follows a slow learning curve before it delivers productivity gains. When the next invention arrives before adopters reach the harvesting phase, it destroys the embedded know-how they had built. The innovator captures the patent prize but ignores this social cost. In a fertile innovation environment, firms innovate too frequently — perpetually restarting the learning curve. Aggregate productivity stagnates precisely because innovation effort is high.

A financial amplification channel deepens the distortion: high returns to R&D-intensive firms relax financing constraints, channeling yet more capital into invention and pushing realized innovation rates further above the social optimum.

The Treadmill
How Silicon Valley's Winning Streak Is Starving the Real Economy

In the spring of 2014, a small robotics company in Fremont, California, was finally getting good at what it did. The workers knew the machines intimately — which joints stuck in cold weather, which shortcuts shaved eleven seconds off a pallet rotation. Throughput was up twenty-two percent. Error rates were cut in half. Then a venture-backed startup unveiled a newer system. Within a year, Meridian was gone — and all that hard-won knowledge evaporated with it.

This is a story that has played out hundreds of times across the American economy, and it goes a long way toward explaining one of the strangest puzzles in modern economics: why the most innovative nation on earth has stopped getting more productive.
